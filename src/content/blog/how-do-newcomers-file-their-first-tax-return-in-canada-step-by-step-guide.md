## Why You Should File a Tax Return in Canada

Most newcomers who were Canadian residents for any part of the calendar year must file a [T1 return](https://www.canada.ca/en/revenue-agency/services/forms-publications/tax-packages-years/general-income-tax-benefit-package.html) if they received income, want to claim a refund, or want to access benefits like the [GST/HST credit](https://www.canada.ca/en/revenue-agency/services/forms-publications/publications/rc4210.html) or [Canada Child Benefit](https://www.canada.ca/en/revenue-agency/services/child-family-benefits/canada-child-benefit.html). Even if you earned nothing, filing triggers your eligibility for benefit payments. [The deadline is **April 30**](https://www.canada.ca/en/revenue-agency/services/tax/individuals/topics/important-dates-individuals.html) for most individuals (June 15 if self-employed, but any balance owing is still due April 30).

## How to File Your Canadian Tax Return: Step-by-Step

### Step 1 — Establish your residency date.

Your residency start date is generally the day you arrived in Canada with the intention of establishing [residential ties](https://www.canada.ca/en/revenue-agency/services/tax/international-non-residents/information-been-moved/determining-your-residency-status.html): a home, family, bank accounts, health card, or driver's license. This date determines your province of residence on December 31 and whether the [90% rule](https://unifysocial.ca/blog/when-newcomers-can-claim-full-non-refundable-tax-credits-in-canada-the-90-rule-explained) applies.

### Step 2 — Gather all income slips.

Collect every T-slip from Canadian sources — your [T4](https://www.canada.ca/en/revenue-agency/services/tax/individuals/topics/about-your-tax-return/tax-return/completing-a-tax-return/tax-slips/understand-your-tax-slips/t4-slips/t4-statement-remuneration-paid.html) from your employer, [T5](https://www.canada.ca/en/revenue-agency/services/tax/individuals/topics/about-your-tax-return/tax-return/completing-a-tax-return/tax-slips/understand-your-tax-slips/t5-slips.html) from your bank, and [T2202](https://www.canada.ca/en/revenue-agency/services/forms-publications/forms/t2202.html) from your school. For income earned before your arrival date, you may need to disclose it on [Schedule A (Statement of World Income)](https://www.canada.ca/en/revenue-agency/services/forms-publications/tax-packages-years/general-income-tax-benefit-package.html) if you do not meet the [90% rule](https://unifysocial.ca/blog/when-newcomers-can-claim-full-non-refundable-tax-credits-in-canada-the-90-rule-explained).

### Step 3 — Choose your filing method.

[CRA-certified NETFILE software](https://www.canada.ca/en/services/taxes/income-tax/personal-income-tax/how-file/tax-software/find-software.html) is the fastest method — free options include [Wealthsimple Tax](https://www.wealthsimple.com/en-ca), [GenuTax](https://www.genutax.ca/), and [UFile](https://www.ufile.ca/) (for lower-income filers). For complex situations involving foreign assets over $100,000 CAD, business income, or rental property, consider a [tax professional](https://www.canada.ca/en/revenue-agency/services/tax/individuals/community-volunteer-income-tax-program.html).

### Step 4 — Complete your T1 return.

Enter your worldwide income for the full year using the [T1 General package for your province](https://www.canada.ca/en/revenue-agency/services/forms-publications/tax-packages-years/general-income-tax-benefit-package.html). On page 1, enter your province of residence on December 31 and your arrival date. If you have foreign income, complete the relevant schedules and, if required, [Form T1135](https://www.canada.ca/en/revenue-agency/services/forms-publications/forms/t1135.html).

### Step 5 — File and register for CRA My Account.

Submit via NETFILE or mail, then register for [CRA My Account](https://www.canada.ca/en/revenue-agency/services/e-services/cra-login-services.html) to track your [Notice of Assessment](https://www.canada.ca/en/services/taxes/income-tax/personal-income-tax/after-you-file/noa-nor.html), set up [direct deposit](https://www.canada.ca/en/revenue-agency/services/about-canada-revenue-agency-cra/direct-deposit.html), and access benefit information.

## Common Tax Filing Mistakes First-Timers Usually Make

1. Missing the arrival date on the T1 — it affects residency status, the [90% rule](https://unifysocial.ca/blog/when-newcomers-can-claim-full-non-refundable-tax-credits-in-canada-the-90-rule-explained) calculation, and credit eligibility.
2. Newcomers commonly forget to report foreign income (including foreign pensions or rental income), since [CRA requires worldwide income disclosure](https://www.canada.ca/en/revenue-agency/services/tax/international-non-residents/information-been-moved/foreign-reporting.html) even if that income is exempt under a [tax treaty](https://www.canada.ca/en/department-finance/programs/tax-policy/tax-treaties.html).
3. Don't forget to apply for the **GST/HST credit** and **Canada Child Benefit** when filing. *(The Canada Child Benefit alone can be worth over $7,000 per child annually!)*
