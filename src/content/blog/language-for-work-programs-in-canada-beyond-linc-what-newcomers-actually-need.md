**Who this is for:** Newcomers at any language level who want to understand Canada's full language training system, from foundational LINC classes to advanced occupation-specific programs that prepare you for professional workplace communication in your industry.

## What are Language for Work Programs and Why Do They Matter?

When newcomers think about language training in Canada, they think about [LINC (Language Instruction for Newcomers to Canada)](https://www.canada.ca/en/immigration-refugees-citizenship/services/settle-canada/language-skills.html). LINC is the right starting point for most newcomers, but it's only part of the picture. For newcomers with intermediate English or French, more targeted occupation-specific programs are available.

Canada has a layered language training system — with different levels, durations, and specializations — that fits newcomers wherever they are in their journey. This post covers the full landscape:

- What LINC is and isn't
- How to know when you are ready to move beyond it
- Occupation-specific programs for newcomers who need professional workplace communication in Canada

## Understanding the Canadian Language Benchmarks (CLB)

All programs in this post use the [Canadian Language Benchmarks (CLB)](https://www.language.ca/resourcesexpertise/on-clb/) scale to place learners and set eligibility. The CLB is the national standard for assessing listening, reading, writing, and speaking. Here's what you need to know:

| CLB Level | Stage | Employment Implications |
|-----------|-------|------------------------|
| CLB 1–4 | Beginner, Basic survival | Entry-level positions, limited communication |
| CLB 5–6 | Low Intermediate, Norms for daily life | Entry-level professional work possible; some employment services open |
| CLB 7–8 | Intermediate, Most everyday communication | Entry-level professional positions; eligible for occupation-specific programs |
| CLB 9–10 | Upper Intermediate, Professional communication possible | Competition for professional roles |
| CLB 10+ | Advanced | Competition for all professional roles |

If you do not know your CLB level, a free assessment is available through any Language Instruction for Newcomers to Canada (LINC) program provider.

## Language Instruction for Newcomers to Canada (LINC)

Canada's federally funded, free language training program — the entry point for the Canadian language training system.

**Who it is for:**

- Adult permanent residents or refugees aged 18 years or older
- Government-sponsored refugees are eligible in all provinces and territories
- Convention refugees are not eligible
- Canadian citizens are not eligible
- No educational level is required for enrollment

The curriculum includes:

- Language instruction from CLB levels 1 through 8, covering listening, reading, writing, and speaking
- A higher CLB (8 or 9): the curriculum includes Canadian workplace communication and some job-specific language training
- LINC providers offer on-site childcare, making the program accessible to parents
- Fully integrated into the settlement system — providers can connect you to other settlement services

**Cost:** Free. Funded by [Immigration, Refugees and Citizenship Canada (IRCC)](https://www.canada.ca/en/immigration-refugees-citizenship.html).

- Settlement.org LINC Locator (Ontario): [settlement.org](https://settlement.org)
- IRCC Language Programs: [canada.ca/en/immigration-refugees-citizenship/services/settle-canada/learn-english-french.html](https://www.canada.ca/en/immigration-refugees-citizenship/services/settle-canada/learn-english-french.html)

## [Occupation-Specific Language Training (OSLT) — Ontario](https://co-oslt.org/)

Ontario's provincially funded language training program for newcomers with professional or workplace experience from their home countries.

**Who it is for:**

- Newcomers with international work experience and education
- Those applying for entry-level to professional jobs in a specific industry in Canada
- The right program for newcomers at the early stages of language learning — CLB 6 and above

**Key benefits:**

- Sector-specific vocabulary is not included in many mainstream language learning programs — OSLT is specifically designed to fill that gap
- Training is offered in the language and format of the specific profession
- Networking: Many programs offer industry-specific networking opportunities

**Duration and format:** Most programs are 8–16 weeks. Delivered in person or online. Many programs are offered part-time, which allows participants to work simultaneously.

**Funding:** Fully subsidized by the province. No cost to participants.

**How to apply or learn more:** [Contact an Employment Ontario](https://www.ontario.ca/page/employment-ontario) service provider in your area.

- ACCES Employment Language for Work: [accesemployment.ca/programs/english-for-the-workplace](https://accesemployment.ca/programs/english-for-the-workplace)
- COSTI Language Training: [costi.org](https://costi.org)
- George Brown College Newcomer Services: [georgebrown.ca](https://georgebrown.ca)

## Enhanced Advanced Language Program (EALP) — British Columbia

BC's provincially funded language training program for newcomers with strong international experience or who are applying to the BC professional labour market.

**Who it is for:**

- Newcomers in British Columbia with professional experience
- Those who have or are looking for more advanced language training for a professional role
- Delivered through colleges, institutes, and secondary institutions
- Open to all permanent residents, including refugees and asylum seekers

The curriculum includes:

- Advanced listening and writing skills
- Language training linked to professional communication
- Worldwide curriculum that can be taken online
- Integration with BC career services

**Duration and format:** 15–26 weeks; in-person or blended delivery. Worksite placements possible when applicable.

**Cost:** Free. Funded as an IRCC program or as a BC program.

- WelcomeBC Language and Skills Training: [welcomebc.ca/Start-Your-Life-in-B-C/Services-for-Newcomers/Language-and-Skills-Training](https://welcomebc.ca/Start-Your-Life-in-B-C/Services-for-Newcomers/Language-and-Skills-Training)

Resources for newcomers settling in provinces outside Ontario and BC.

| Alberta | NorQuest College | Occupation-specific and settlement language training |

**Key benefits by province:**

- **Alberta:** Immigrant language and settlement programs offered through different organizations
- **Cost:** Free or subsidized; varies by program and delivery location

**Cost:** Free. Provincially funded.

**How to apply or learn more:** Contact the local immigrant services organization in your city. [Use IRCC's settlement services finder](https://ircc.canada.ca/english/newcomers/services/index.asp) to find language programs in your area.

## How the Language Training Fits the Broader Career Journey

Language training is part of a larger career development pathway. The programs in this post all map to specific career stages:

| Stage | Program Type | Language Level |
|-------|-------------|---------------|
| Arrival | LINC 1–8 | CLB 1–8 |
| Job Search | OSLT or EALP | CLB 7 |
| First Role | OSLT or EALP | CLB 8 |
| Career Advancement | Employer English; Sector | CLB 9+ |

The general principle: advance your language level to match (or precede) your career goals. Many newcomers move into job search mode before their language level qualifies them for professional roles. Programs such as OSLT are not well recognized by all employers — document your participation to demonstrate your commitment.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**If you have professional-level English (CLB 8+), will assessments still apply?**

For most jobs, your professional English level is what matters. The OSLT and EALP programs are specifically for newcomers who are working in a profession and want to advance their sector-specific communication skills.

**Is employment experience required for OSLT?**

Yes. Employment experience in Canada or abroad is required for most OSLT providers. These programs look for candidates who have the potential for job placement, so demonstrating employment history is important.

**Are these employment programs?**

Not directly. They are employment readiness and language programs, meaning they support professional development rather than directly placing you in a role. These programs look for candidates who have the potential for job placement, so demonstrating employment history is important.
